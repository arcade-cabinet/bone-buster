# Rebrand — from `objexoom` to its arcade-cabinet identity

## Why

OBJEXOOM is the working title that survived the extraction from
`objexiv/objexiv@feat/objexoom-easter-egg` on 2026-05-13 (see
[`CLAUDE.md`](../CLAUDE.md)). The name was a one-off joke
("Objexiv's DOOM mod"), funny in context but unintelligible
outside it. Now that the game has reached 100% reference-clone
parity + 13 elevation phases + Phase 21 perf hardening, it
deserves an identity that:

1. Is **alliterative** + **fun** — matches the arcade-cabinet
   house style (`Echoes of Beastlight`, `Otter River Rush`,
   `Protocol Silent Night`, `Rivers of Reckoning`).
2. Is **unique** + **searchable** — no Google collision with
   established id-Software / Bethesda IP.
3. Carries its own **visual/audio identity**, NOT a discount-
   DOOM cosplay. The PSX 3D asset aesthetic + R6/playful
   neon-grid HUD + retro-arcade terminal type is its own
   thing now; the name should signal that.
4. Reads as **fun-first**, not edgy-first. The user's
   reference (`Big Baboom`) is a clue: onomatopoeic,
   playful, sounds like an arcade machine going off, not a
   1993 IRC channel.

## Tonal corner-pins

The game IS:
- A first-person arcade shooter played in short bursts
- Goofy in the PSX-jank way (skinned skeletons that wobble,
  wraiths that phase walls, vehicle wrecks in the
  courtyard archetype)
- Procedurally generated with 5 distinct archetype "biomes"
  (corridor / arena / courtyard / sewer / library)
- Built around a "rip and tear → find key → escape" loop
- Mobile-targeted (Capacitor, Android emulator perf gate)

The game IS NOT:
- A reverent DOOM tribute
- A horror game (despite the skeletons)
- Edgy / mature / "gritty"
- A roguelike (one continuous run, not meta-progression)

## NAME LOCKED — `Bone Buster`

User picked **Bone Buster** from the candidate list. Two
words, B+B alliterative, "bone" tags the skeleton-heavy
enemy roster + the brittle PSX aesthetic, "buster" tags the
pistol/chaingun/shotgun kineticism. The name is on-the-nose
about what the game IS: you bust bones.

This means the prior "DOOM-shape one-word" candidates
(GRIND, GULCH, RUST etc) are dropped from this doc.

### Why it works

- **Names the activity.** Player picks up the game, sees
  "Bone Buster", knows in one second that they're going to
  shoot skeletons. DOOM names a feeling; Bone Buster names
  an action.
- **Self-aware playfulness.** "Buster" is goofy on purpose
  — Ghostbusters / Block Buster / monster-of-the-week. The
  PSX-jank skeletons can't carry a "serious horror" name;
  Bone Buster gives them permission to wobble.
- **Alliterative + scannable.** B+B reads fast on a marquee.
  10 letters total + one space. Cabinet-marquee shape.
- **No AAA collision.** A 2026 search shows no major game
  with this exact name.
- **Lends itself to a tagline:** `Bone Buster — keep
  busting.` or `Bone Buster — they had it coming.`

### Original one-word candidates retained for the historical
### record (not selected)

User considered one-word options like DOOM-shape iconic
names; the final pick was the two-word **Bone Buster**. The
one-word brainstorm pool (kept for posterity) included: RUST,
GRIND, GULCH, SLAB, CHURN, HUSK, GAUNT, CLANG, PYRE, KILN,
THRESH, MAUL, GUNK, BRAZE, CLEAVE, GUILD, OSSUARY, CRYPT,
SCRAP, HEW, JAW, VOID.

### Old one-word constraint framing (for future reference)

The name should:

- Be 1 word, 4-7 letters ideally, monosyllable preferred
- Evoke a feeling, not literally describe the gameplay
- Be Google-unique in 2026 (no AAA collisions)
- Look good in 6-foot-tall arcade-cabinet typography
- Sound like a thing you'd yell

### Tier 1 — the candidates that hit hardest

| Name | Why it lands | Tagline pitch |
|---|---|---|

| **GRIND** | 5 letters, monosyllable, iconic. "Grind" is the chaingun, the arcade loop, the procedural repetition; the verb you do; the noise the game makes. Skater/streetwear connotations are a feature not a bug — leans into the playful-irreverent corner. No AAA collision. | "Keep going." |
| **GULCH** | 5 letters, monosyllable, weird in a way DOOM was weird. A gulch is a deep narrow ravine — matches the procedural-corridor shape AND has the Wild-West-with-skeletons reading the PSX aesthetic carries. Almost zero prior-art. Sounds like a place. | "Down you go." |
| **SLAB** | 4 letters, monosyllable, brutalist. A slab is a flat wall, a tombstone, a chunk of concrete, a corpse on the table. Every wall in the procedural maps is a slab. Reads as architectural, blunt, and a little funereal. No collision. | "Walls down." |
| **CHURN** | 5 letters, monosyllable. Churn = the act of grinding, the act of mixing (procedural rooms), the noise of a chaingun, butter from milk, blood from skeletons. The word does heavy semantic work for one syllable. No major collision. | "Mix and match the skeletons." |
| **HUSK** | 4 letters, monosyllable. Husk = empty shell, what's left of a skeleton, what the PSX-low-poly enemies LOOK like (hollow, simplified). Strong identity for the enemy roster. Slightly horror-adjacent but the PSX-jank takes the edge off. | "Empty everything." |
| **GAUNT** | 5 letters, monosyllable. Gauntlet vibes without being literally "Gauntlet" (which is taken by the 1985 arcade classic — collision risk). Sound: hard G + nasal T = arcade-cabinet shape. Evokes the procedural-room procession. | "Through the rooms." |
| **CLANG** | 5 letters, monosyllable. Pure onomatopoeia — the sound of a pistol round on a skeleton's ribcage, the door slamming open, the key landing on its pedestal. The most fun-first option in this tier. No collision. | "Hit something." |

### Long-shot one-word weird

- **OSSUARY** — bone repository. 3 syllables (too long?) but literally
  describes the library archetype's bookshelves-of-bones reading.
- **CRYPT** — clean 5 letters, monosyllable, but locks the game into
  one biome's identity (the library) and is overused
- **SCRAP** — junkyard aesthetic, matches vehicle wrecks +
  flamethrower-melts-everything-into-scrap reading
- **HEW** — 3 letters! Verb meaning "to chop", classic medieval-axe word.
  Almost too short to scan visually
- **JAW** — 3 letters, body horror evoked, but maybe too literal
- **VOID** — 4 letters, monosyllable, perfect arcade shape — but
  overused in 2020s indie naming

## Recommendation

**GRIND** is the strongest pick for this game specifically:

- **It IS the loop.** Procedural rooms, chaingun, find the key,
  reach the portal, next level, repeat — the player IS grinding.
  The word names what the game IS.
- **Monosyllabic, 5 letters**, scans like DOOM scans. Hard
  consonants (G + D), one nasal in the middle. Yells well.
- **Self-aware** — calling it GRIND is a wink at the genre.
  DOOM was named for dread; GRIND is named for the repetition.
  That's identity, not derivative.
- **Skater/punk visual lineage** unlocks the redesign you
  asked for — SVG shape-letters, animated scuff/scratch
  shaders, framer-motion-driven "lock-in" entry, Radix-
  powered menus that read as DIY zine cuts instead of
  AAA UX.
- **Tagline:** `Keep going.` — 2 words, evocative, doesn't
  literally describe gameplay, leaves room for the player.

**Runner-ups:**
- **GULCH** if you want geographic / place identity (deep
  ravine where skeletons live) — Wild-West-with-bones reading
- **CHURN** if you want the mechanical-process identity
- **CLANG** if you want pure onomatopoeia / sound-first
- **HUSK** if you want enemy-roster-led identity

## Typography — Google Fonts pairing

The current `ObjexoomLanding` uses what looks like a CSS-
gradient stencil treatment of a generic display font. For
Bone Buster's identity (playful + B-movie + pistol-rounds-on-
ribcage), I'm proposing a **3-font system** all from Google
Fonts:

| Role | Font | Why |
|---|---|---|
| **Display** (BONE BUSTER logo, screen titles like "MISSION COMPLETE", level-name HUD chip) | **Bungee** | Designed by David Jonathan Ross specifically for urban signage. Boxy, condensed, ALL-CAPS by design (it doesn't have lowercase glyphs — perfect, the game never needs them in headlines). Reads as carnival/arcade/luchador-poster all at once. The B's in BUNGEE and BONE BUSTER share the same chunky personality. Free, OFL-licensed. |
| **Body** (HUD readouts, menu items, in-game tooltips, "FIND THE KEY" prompts) | **Space Grotesk** | Modern geometric sans designed by Florian Karsten. Slight humanist warmth keeps the HUD from feeling sterile. Pairs cleanly with Bungee without competing — Bungee shouts, Space Grotesk talks. Free, SIL OFL. |
| **Mono** (debug overlay `FPS 60 · DPR 1.50`, score numerals, level names like `E1M1`, secret coordinates) | **JetBrains Mono** | Already in the design-token vocabulary of half the games in the arcade-cabinet org. Open ligatures, slightly playful zero with the slash. Free, OFL. |

Single bonus font for one specific moment: **Tilt Prism** by
Andrew Footit for the GAME OVER / MISSION COMPLETE
animated-title slot — it's a variable-axis font where the
glyphs can rotate in 3D space via `font-variation-settings`,
which framer-motion can drive. Use sparingly — only at
phase-transition moments where the type performs.

All four are weight + variable-axis fonts (Bungee Spice,
Bungee Inline, Bungee Shade siblings give us "stickered"
and "outlined" variants of the logo type without a new
type purchase).

CSS bootstrap (one `<link>` per family or a single
`https://fonts.googleapis.com/css2?family=Bungee&family=Bungee+Inline&family=Bungee+Shade&family=JetBrains+Mono:wght@400;600&family=Space+Grotesk:wght@400;500;700&family=Tilt+Prism&display=swap` call). We can self-host
via `@fontsource/*` packages instead if the asset budget
allows — better LCP, no third-party DNS hit. The CSP we
shipped in S2 currently disallows external font loads
(`font-src 'self' data:`); keep that strict and use
@fontsource (recommended).

cyber on dark navy. For Bone Buster we want a palette that
reads as **bone, blood, neon-night-club, brass-knuckle** all
at once. Industrial but playful. NOT horror.

The brand gradient on the logo runs `bone1 → bone2 → bone3`
diagonally (top-left to bottom-right), with `blood` as the
solid stroke around the letters. The orange `accent.primary`

the logo itself, which keeps the logo (warm white-cream) and
the gameplay UI (warm orange) visually distinct so the HUD
chip never gets confused for branding.

The 5 archetype lighting palettes (`archetypePalette.ts`)
get a one-pass refresh to shift their accent tints toward
this new palette. Corridor stays violet-leaning (matches
`accent.discovery`), arena gets the danger-crimson tint,
courtyard pulls the gain-mint, sewer pulls warning-amber,
library pulls the bone-tan/brown band. Visual gate
canonical screenshots will refresh as part of the
refresh PR.

## Enemy rename — align with Bone Buster

User noted: "make sur the enemies all align or give them new
names alongside". Current enemy roster is `skeleton`,
`wraith`, `imp` (DOOM/D&D taxonomy). For Bone Buster:

| Old | New | Why |
|---|---|---|
| `skeleton` | `rattler` | Already a skeleton; the bones rattle when it walks (the PSX wobble animation reads as bone-rattle). "Rattler" sounds like a B-movie kaiju name. Genre-bends without losing the visual identity — the model stays identical. |
| `wraith` | `phaser` | "Phaser" because it phases through walls (its existing mechanic) — names the mechanic, not the lore. Strips the DOOM/D&D framing while keeping the gameplay meaning visible to the player. |
| `imp` | `bouncer` | The imp's current behavior is fast-and-aggressive hit-and-run. "Bouncer" frames it as a nightclub-bouncer kicking the player out, leans into the playful B-movie tone, and reads kinetic. The model stays identical (it's a horned humanoid; could just as easily be a club enforcer with horns). |

The roster identity stays **3 enemies, 3 distinct movement
mechanics**. The rename is metadata + HUD-strings + sub-
agent-killed events; the GLB assets + AI behavior don't
change. The directive item below (D4) covers the
EnemyKind type alias + every consuming switch statement.

Bosses + ambient NPCs are not renamed — they're already
generic ("boss", "npc").

### Optional follow-on — flavor names for variants

Each enemy has a "roster" of skin variants (5-8 GLBs per
kind, see `src/models.ts` `ENEMY_MODELS`). After D4, we
could add per-variant flavor names that surface in the
death-confirmation popup or boss-kill ceremony:
"You busted a Rattler (Cracked-Skull variant)." Phase 23
polish item, not Phase 22.

## Asset-usage audit — what's already available but barely shipped

User noted: "youre also missing several other enemies i gave you
in references hiw come all the stuff i gave you is barely used?".

The current shipped enemy model file count is 11 distinct GLBs
in `public/assets/models/enemies/horror/` + 3 base files —
abomination, abomination2, alien, anomaly, clown_1, clown_3,
elk_demon, horned, nun, plague_doctor, sewerfiend, plus
skeleton.glb / imp.glb / wraith.glb.

But the runtime ENEMY_MODELS treats all of these as **skin
variants** of just 3 enemy kinds (skeleton, imp, wraith) — so
each model is cosmetic only. The player encounters a
"skeleton" and might see it rendered as a plague_doctor or
nun on one map and an actual skeleton on the next, but
behavior is identical and the HUD never tells them
they're different.

**This is the gap.** The PSX-asset library is providing 11
distinct visual identities and we're collapsing them to 3
behavioral identities.

### Proposed promotion path (D5 in Phase 22)

Promote some of the current "skin variants" to **first-class
EnemyKind** entries with their own stats + behavior tuning.
The bones stay (no new AI architecture); we add config
rows to `ENEMY_MODELS`, `enemyBaseHp`, the spawn-mix tables,
and the per-archetype mix lookup.

| Old name | New first-class kind | Inherits movement from | Distinguishing trait |
|---|---|---|---|
| `skeleton` | `rattler` (renamed) | skeleton (slow melee) | Bone — vulnerable to BLADE |
| `wraith` | `phaser` (renamed) | wraith (phases walls) | Cold — vulnerable to FLAMETHROWER |
| `imp` | `bouncer` (renamed) | imp (fast aggressive) | Quick — vulnerable to SHOTGUN |
| `plague_doctor` (was imp variant) | **`plaguebeak`** (new kind) | imp behavior, slower | Carrier — leaves a slow-damage gas cloud on death |
| `clown_1` / `clown_3` (was imp variants) | **`jester`** (new kind) | imp behavior, erratic | Trickster — fires three shots in a fan instead of one |
| `nun` (was skeleton variant) | **`reverend`** (new kind) | skeleton behavior, ranged | Ranged — fires from a distance instead of charging |
| `elk_demon` (was imp variant) | **`stagged`** (new kind) | imp behavior, charging | Bull-rush — straight-line charge that's deadly head-on, easy to sidestep |
| `sewerfiend` (was skeleton variant) | **`grub`** (new kind) | skeleton melee, low HP, fast | Numerical — spawns in groups of 3-5 in sewer archetype only |
| `alien` (was wraith variant) | **`signal`** (new kind) | wraith behavior, ranged | Anti-air — only enemy that targets the player even through walls |
| `abomination` / `abomination2` (were imp variants) | **`heap`** / **`heap2`** (renamed pair) | imp tank variant | Heavy — high HP, slow melee, drops health pickup on death |
| `horned` (was skeleton variant) | **`gorehead`** (new kind) | skeleton melee, bigger | Charge-attack on line of sight |

So instead of 3 enemy kinds (skeleton/imp/wraith), we get
**11 distinct kinds** (rattler + phaser + bouncer + plaguebeak +
jester + reverend + stagged + grub + signal + heap + gorehead).

Each kind needs:
- One row in `EnemyKind` type + `ENEMY_MODELS`
- A baseHp + damage in `enemyBaseHp` / damage tables
- A spawn-weight in the per-archetype mix tables
  (`src/enemyMix.ts`)
- A vulnerability tag (used by D6 below to surface "use the
  RIGHT weapon" gameplay)
- Optional: a one-line flavor description that surfaces in
  the kill-confirmation popup (Phase 23 polish)
